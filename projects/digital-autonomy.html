Gen Z's Digital Autonomy

Investigating how social media platform design impacts autonomy in Gen Z users and translating research insights into design opportunities that support intentional use.

INFO 464: Value Sensitive Design Team Project Role: UX Researcher / Designer

Overview

Social media platforms often optimize for engagement at the cost of users' well-being. For Gen Z — the demographic that spends the most time on these platforms — this can show up as attention fatigue, compulsive scrolling, and a growing mismatch between how people want to use social media and how they actually do.

Our team studied how Gen Z experiences autonomy on social media, what conditions lead to intentional versus compulsive engagement, and how design might support autonomy without feeling restrictive or punitive.

My Contribution

  • Led the design of the intent-mode wireframes, translating research insights into concrete interface concepts
  • Conducted 2 diary studies and 2 semi-structured interviews with Gen Z participants
  • Synthesized findings to support the project's core framing: autonomy as alignment between intent and content

Problem

Many social platforms use emotionally triggering, recommendation-heavy patterns to keep users scrolling. This can reduce autonomy, create attention fatigue, and push usage into compulsive patterns.

Research Questions

  • How do Gen Z users experience autonomy while using social media?
  • What situations lead to intentional versus compulsive engagement?
  • How might we design solutions that promote user autonomy?

Methodology

We used a Tripartite Methodology across empirical, conceptual, and technical phases — combining lived experience research with values-focused analysis and design exploration.

Empirical

  • Diary Study
  • Semi-Structured Interviews

Conceptual

Key Findings

Self-moderation breaks down

People often ignore their own attempts at screen-time limits, especially when usage is habitual or emotionally driven.

Intentional → compulsive is common

Sessions often start with a purpose and gradually shift into autopilot scrolling.

Habit loops + app design amplify overuse

Users described routines (morning/night rotations), and recognized that short-form recommended content “hooks” them in.

Overload and mis-estimation

People underestimate passive scrolling and can feel anxiety and emotional instability from information overload.

Design Framing

A key reframing emerged from our synthesis:

Autonomy ≠ usage level. It’s about intent and content.

Users wanted support that “works with you” rather than rigid time-based restrictions. That pushed us toward designs that help users clarify their purpose and stay aligned with it — without surveillance or judgment.

Design Concepts

We explored multiple directions that support autonomy through transparency, intentionality, and reduced compulsion.

Personalized data visualizations

Make patterns visible so users can reflect on what they consume — and whether it aligns with their values and goals.

Algorithm sensitivity / friction options

Give users control over recommendation intensity and “hook” patterns, nudging toward natural stopping points.

Progressive awareness program

Build autonomy through supportive conditions over time — aiming for user independence rather than system reliance.

Takeaways

  • Autonomy is supported best when users can specify intent and reflect on content.
  • Transparency builds self-awareness and reduces reliance on blunt time-limit tools.
  • Design should shift feeds toward patterns that naturally reduce compulsive engagement.

Future Work

  • Usability test the wireframes with Gen Z users and iterate based on feedback.
  • Explore how these features could be integrated into existing platforms.
  • Refine concepts into a more cohesive hi-fi prototype direction.
